The Effects of Solitary Confinement on Mental Health

The effects of solitary confinement on mental health

Solitary confinement is a widely debated topic in our criminal justice system alongside the death penalty. There is a controversy surrounding the morality of isolating a human for long periods. This method of punishment is growing in the United States as an estimated 80,000 to 100,000 inmates are held in solitary confinement ("Solitary confinement"). Some inmates can see up to 22 hours to 24 hours in isolation for sometimes up to a month with minimal meaningful interaction with others ("Solitary confinement"). The isolation in itself could cause numerous mental health issues but the deprival of human interaction also negatively impacts these inmates. These individuals often get denied contact with family or visitors, despite family to have been shown as an important factor in successful rehabilitation ("Solitary confinement"). Inmates in isolation are placed in an environment where mental deterioration is expected. One inmate, Jordan, described his experience of going into the SHU, security housing unit when he was 16. He reported feeling 'changed' as if he "aged ten years just by doing 20 days in there" (Eldridge, 2018).

Jordan stated he had nothing to do but think about everything in his life and a majority of the time his thoughts were about the negatives about his ongoing case and troubles at home (Eldridge, 2018). For young inmates in the SHU, they receive no sort of teaching or schooling during an important developmental phase as a young adult. He wasn't allowed to attend programs while in there so a teacher would come twice a week and bring a folder filled with worksheets, articles, and puzzles. When they wouldn't even collect the work he did he asked "Well, what did you bring it for?... Some of this stuff I needed help on, some of this stuff in here I didn't understand"(Eldridge, 2018). When Jordan got out of the SHU he reported that it was difficult for him to readjust to human interaction because he was so used to being alone; he felt the only person there for him was himself (Eldridge, 2018).

Solitary can effect not only your personality, mental health, and in Jordan's case education. Jordan had felt changed. He believes he is now more in tune with himself because of solitary where he had plenty of time to get to know himself (Eldridge, 2018). Medical research has proven that the denial of meaningful human contact can lead to 'isolation syndrome' or 'solitary confinement syndrome'. Some of the noted symptoms are hyperresponsivity to external stimuli, perceptual distortions, hallucinations, derealization experiences, problems with impulse control, difficulty with concentrating and memory, increased anxiety, depression, anger, paranoia, psychosis, self-harm, and suicide ("Solitary confinement"; Grassian, 1983). Solitary confinement has been proven to alter neural and therefore psychological states causing these individuals (28%) to have higher rates of psychopathologies than the general population (15%) (Rodriguez, 2011). Prisoners in isolation have also been statistically shown to engage in self-mutilation at higher rates than those of the general population (Rodriguez, 2011). Self-mutilation is typically a warning sign of suicide. Solitary confinement has a high correlation to suicide rates. In 2005, within the California prison system, 70% of forty-four prisoners who had committed suicide were in solitary confinement (Rodriguez, 2011). Craig Haney, a psychology professor from the University of California Santa Cruz, has spent the past thirty years studying solitary confinement units and their long-term effects on prisoners. Haney highlighted the core irrationalities in the logic of solitary confinement:

these units make the ability to withstand the psychological assault of extreme isolation a prerequisite for allowing prisoners to return to the intensely social world of mainline prison or free society. In this way, prisoners who cannot 'handle' the profound isolation of supermax confinement are almost always doomed to be retained in it. And those who have adapted all too well to the deprivation, restriction, and pervasive control are prime candidates for release to a social world to which they may be incapable of ever fully readjusting

-Craig Haney, Mental Health Issues in Long-Term Solitary and Supermax Confinement

Solitary confinement teaches problematic psychological habits to prisoners. The logic is irrational and promotes psychological distress to reenter society. In addition to the negative psychological effects of solitary, there is another important mental health issue raised by these conditions of the number of mentally ill prisoners who get placed in isolation.

Mental illness in solitary confinement

The use of solitary confinement is justified by states as a disciplinary punishment for prisoners or to protect other prisoners. As a part of this, prison staff uses solitary to help 'manage' certain individuals ("Solitary confinement"). These certain individuals more than often include prisoners who have or are perceived to have mental illnesses. The symptoms of mental illness in prison, such as being noisy or disruptive, refusing orders, self-mutilating, or attempting suicide can cause prison staff to house the mentally ill in isolation pushing them over the edge to psychosis ("U.S.: Number of Mentally Ill in Prisons Quadrupled", 2006). The harsh setting of isolation worsens the mental conditions of these prisoners making it hard to ever receive treatment. Also, as their symptoms get worse this makes them more prone to the behaviors that got them put into solitary to begin with, resulting in longer and more frequent stays in isolation. A 2003 report from the Human Rights Watch found that one-third to one-half of prisoners in isolation suffered from some mental illness (Rodriguez, 2011). Placing these individuals in isolation further deteriorates their mental health while also restricting their access to any programs they may need for treatment.

What is being done or can be done?

The United Nations Human Rights had declared that for juveniles, all cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment is strictly prohibited including "placement in a dark cell, closed or solitary confinement or any other punishment that may compromise the physical or mental health of the juvenile concerned" (UNHR, 1990). Solitary confinement has been recognized as a cruel and inhumane treatment for juveniles by the United Nations and should also be for adults. Solitary confinement conflicts with the eighth amendment preventing the infliction of cruel and unusual punishments. Solitary confinement increases the risk of torture or ill-treatment going unnoticed and in itself can represent torture ("Solitary confinement"). Solitary encourages maltreatment of prisoners and should only be used as a last resort and possibly only for the most dangerous prisoners. When used though, prisons should ensure that meaningful human interactions are available. This can be done by introducing more staff-prisoner contact, access to social activities with other prisoners, visits, and in-depth talks with mental health professionals and religious personnel ("Solitary confinement"). Craig Haney also proposed that research is critical to analyze, modify, and reform the harsh conditions; understanding the psychological impacts would result in the most effective programs for prisoners to cope after isolation (Haney, 2003). He highlights the need for the development of programs that would assist prisoners with "unlearning problematic habits of thinking, feeling, and acting on which their psychological survival in supermax often depends" (Haney, 2003).
