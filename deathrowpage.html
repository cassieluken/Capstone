Death Row and the Mental Strains

What's it like living on death row?

Death row inmates experience the harshest sentencing in our criminal justice system. Death row inmates are typically incarcerated in solitary confinement so the mental health issues tend to overlap. (Click here to see the solitary confinement tab) The death penalty information center recognizes that these inmates are subject to much more deprivation and harsher conditions when compared to other prisoners ("Conditions on Death Row"). These conditions lead to a decline in mental health as they spend extensive time in isolation. Nearly half of the people executed nationwide between 2000 and 2015 had been diagnosed with a mental illness by a professional at some point in their lives ( Roth, 2018). The conditions of isolation and the lengthy time spent on death row acerbates inmates' conditions ("Conditions on Death Row"). The time spent on death row alone could cause mental health issues from tension and suspense. Time can contribute to the worsening of mental health but as of now, long periods are needed to complete the death penalty appeals correctly for inmates ("Conditions on Death Row"). Because of the nature of the death penalty, the system needs to spend time to assure innocent people are not executed to avoid grave injustices for these individuals but this can cause inmates to wait twenty years or more in degrading conditions while their cases are reviewed ("Conditions on Death Row"). So not only is the time spent under these conditions lengthy, but there is a lot more pressure in the reevaluation process. These retrials could be the difference between living or dying which creates a more stressful and depressing environment for these inmates. The stress is high especially for Alabama death row inmates. Alabama has the largest death row per capita in the US and the nation's highest death sentencing rate; additionally, Alabama does not provide legal assistance when inmates want to challenge any inadequate representation they had experienced unlike all other death penalty states ("Alabama's Death Penalty System Generates Growing Criticism", 2016). There is also no state-wide public defender for Alabama. The legal team inmates have is usually a deciding factor of whether or not a defendant will be sentenced to death, creating added pressure to try to get a good legal team when one is not appointed to you ("Alabama's Death Penalty System...", 2016). Those with mental illness are also more "...likely to fire their defense lawyers, or not cooperate with them, or even try to represent themselves” therefore adding more pressure to the retrial process (Chammah, 2019). Alabama has the highest numbers and does not assist inmates, pushing them down a dark, hopeless, and depressive path. Outside of the retrial process, the living conditions are described as toxic. One death row inmate, Paul Brown, describes his experience finding his friend and fellow death row inmate attempting suicide. Brown admitted that he felt that the other inmates, who knew his friend was in a locked closet by himself for nearly 30 minutes, were disappointed and upset with him as if he "were spoiling the fun" since suicide attempts feel welcomed (2018). Brown confessed that it is easier to succumb:

There is no disappointment when there are no expectations. We don't live on death row; we wait to die

-Paul Brown, Should I Have Let My Friend on Death Row Kill Himself?

These inmates experience hopelessness, just waiting for the next inevitable phase of death. Brown describes the environment of death row as constantly reinforcing death "From our clothing (red jumpsuits) to the walls of the unit (also painted blood red), every day is a crime scene. When we go outside, we’re surrounded by guards on high platforms with high-powered rifles pointed at us" (Brown, 2018). The environment and lifestyle had effected Brown enough to get him to question himself for saving his friend and asking if death is preferable to death row (Brown, 2018). Death is the theme of every day for these inmates leaving them to thoughts of suicide. Brown has noticed that "Living on death row is a contradiction. Some become overwhelmed by the perpetual thought of death looming over our heads, the denial of all physical contact with loved ones or any other human beings, and the daily indignities to our personhood. Others fight like hell and cling to the tiniest semblances of life: a letter, a visit, a handshake, a smile" (Brown, 2018). The constant reminder of death and denial of any healing factors like physical contact strip away willpower. Clinging to tiny symbols of life is their last plea of help before they lose hope and come to understand the theme of death row is death. Feelings of being hopeless are linked to depression and affect your mood and thinking ("What Are the Causes and Symptoms of Depression"). Depression and suicide reinforce each other here as people obsess over death. From 1977 through 2010, the rate of suicides on death row averaged 2.74, while the mean per 100,000 was 129.7 annually (Tartaro & Lester, 2016). These numbers, when compared to the rate of suicide outside of prison and for inmates in state prisons at that time, are significantly higher. The general inmate population has a regular prison setting where they spend a majority of the time surrounded by other inmates. For death row inmates they primarily serve in solitary confinement making suicide more possible with the lack of intervention of other inmates and officers and leaving a larger psychological impact of stress and despair when compared to most state prison inmates (Tartaro & Lester, 2016). When California built a psychiatric unit on its death row at San Quentin the 40 beds were filled immediately ( Roth, 2018). The suicide numbers, the differences in conditions, and the theme of death looming in this environment shows the necessity for mental health care for death row inmates. Whether inmates receive psychiatric treatment programs or focus groups, death row inmates deserve additional resources due to the nature of their environment and death sentences.

A call for the ban of the death penalty for the mentally ill

This discussion has mentioned the living conditions and retrial process when already sentenced to the death penalty, but for those who are mentally ill and risk being charged with the death penalty, many also see a problem with this. Heath Otto was diagnosed with schizophrenia and awaits trial for strangling his mother and nephew and then slitting their throats (Chammah, 2019). (See about his case here: ("He’s Living With Severe Mental Illness. Should He Face the Death Penalty? ") The significance of this case is that Otto was ruled as incompetent to stand trial but now the state's attorney may seek the death penalty.

Hover over image to read more about Otto's case. Heath Otto, left, killed his mother, Carol Simon, right, and his 7-year-old nephew. Courtesy of Aleesha DeKnikker.
Otto had claimed, falsely, that his mother and nephew had medical conditions and he intended to “put them out of their misery.” He later said “It was an order from the CIA, and then he said stuff about Hillary Clinton, and if he didn’t do it it was going to be World War III”(Chammah, 2019). Because of his diagnoses, after the incident Otto had to wait over a month for a bed to open in the only public psychiatric hospital in the state. The state attorney has not formally announced that they will be pursuing the death penalty, but Otto's sister, the mother of Otto's nephew, Cassandra Otto has commented "I do forgive him, in a way, because I know my mom tried getting him help,...I’m in a phase where I don’t know if he’s faking it or he’s really mentally ill.” But, she added, “I think he should get the death penalty no matter what, because my family doesn’t deserve what happened to us. My sister disagrees, because that wasn’t her kid." She said her brother “knew what he was doing"(Chammah, 2019).

This process is causing more strain on the family members and between family members than Otto. Otto's other sister, Aleesha DeKnikker, has announced that "killing another member of our family in no way honors my mom's life..." (Chammah, 2019). This process and the threat of the death penalty not only places mental strains on the perpetrators but others as well, whether it be family or friends of the perpetrator or even the victims. A friend of Otto's mother stated:

If he understood what he did to his mother, he would probably try to commit suicide. It’s much easier to kill a mentally ill man than to admit your mental health system, your law enforcement, your policies and procedure are so inept

-Maddie Borah, He’s Living With Severe Mental Illness. Should He Face the Death Penalty?

She sees the entire case as an example of the way our society treats those with severe mental health problems (Chammah, 2019). People with severe mental illness such as schizophrenia and bipolar disorder are considered cognitively impaired when it comes to decision-making ("Mental Illness"). At this point, the American Psychiatric Association and the American Bar Association have called for a ban on the death penalty for those with severe mental illness due to several possibilities: the lack of understanding their crime and its consequences, their impairment in decision-making, and their mental incompetence ("Mental Illness").

What is being done or can be done?

Many legal experts have declared that prolonged isolation is a form of cruel and unusual punishment, comparable to torture ("Conditions on Death Row"). If experts deem the lifestyle of death row inmates as cruel and unusual this violates the eighth amendment and therefore should be abolished. Since time under these conditions causes even further harm, the Death Penalty Information Center (DPIC) provides rules governing inmates on death row in each state and tracks the amount of time inmates spend under these conditions ("Conditions on Death Row"). In this case, time is not easily altered. There still seems to be a struggle to find a way to lessen the time spent on death row, therefore the worsening of mental health issues, while also properly overseeing all death penalty appeals. The whole death penalty sentencing should be reviewed since the conditions cause serious mental health issues. For those who come into their sentence with a mental health issue already, there have been proposals and bills to ban death sentences for people with serious mental health illnesses some including PTSD, and others limited to schizophrenia, schizoaffective disorder and bipolar disorder (Chammah, 2019). The "insanity defense" continues to be narrowly defined and juries are skeptical which then effects the outcome of these trials. The Supreme Court has already banned the death penalty for those with intellectual disabilities so mental health issues should be the next step.
